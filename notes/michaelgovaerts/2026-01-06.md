RE: Hitlist Magic
Don't make constraints too rigid. Common problems: Not going back far enough, being too restrictive on technologies, focusing on only entry/junior roles
- Make sure that you're using the most updated hitlist magic guidelines
- Smaller metro areas can be harder to find leads
-- Expand the search parameters. Go back an entire year. Find the companies that have been hiring recently so that you can focus on those companies (since they likely have funding to hire you) with your hitlisting
RE: Banki
- Everyone can get answers to the Banki questions, regardless of background. Just need to be creative about forming answers
-- ChatGPT tool from HuntOber that should be in the Discord to help us frame our narrative and form good answers
- Come to Banki Brunch or ask on Discord to help craft better answers, especially if we're having problems. Don't ever hesitate to ask for support, especially if we've already put the work in to try to help ourselves. 100Devs is about growing together.
- 100Devs uses "CAR" instead of "STAR". Leon thinks STAR has one too many letters :p
RE: Personal vs. Professional Opinions on AI.
Leon: ""Job asking us to do certain things AI-related?" Not understanding the question."
- Some people have an apprehension to using AI. Leon sees AI as a StackOverflow. Not up in arms about StackOverflow, no personal qualms about AI or using it in his professional day. Leon is pretty AI-positive at this point
- AI is a tool. He's not going to get mad about a hammer.
- If we're an artist or someone who does design work, AI definitely impacts this more.
- If Leon ever gets asked on a job to do something that he feels ethically opposed to, then it's definitely time to find a new job
-- What a time to find one, too, since at least 5 people got jobs during our holiday break!
RE: How to get reference for first tech job?
- Reference can be anyone. Doesn't need to be technical. Anyone that has experience working with us in any capacity
-- Former coworkers. Doesn't even have to be a boss. Just someone who worked with us in a professional capacity. Previous opportunity. Worked on a client project with someone at 100Devs. All of these are fair game for a reference. Up for employer to determine if they don't like the reference. Most of the time, it just needs to be someone who we've worked with in a professional capacity. Commmunity projects or hackathons can work. Just need someone who knows we work well with others and are professionally capable
RE: Navigate full-time job offer when we're doing lots of volunteer work? (Like what Leon does but on a smaller scale)
- Tell job up-front that the volunteer stuff is important and it's beneficial for your personal success, but only if it's during work hours. If it's after work hours, then your time is your time. If it's during work hours, just make sure the employer recognizes the importance, and make sure you make it contingent on your job
- Collaborating with others is very helpful for personal and professional growth, so these opportunities are typically worth keeping (especially, as Leon mentions later, for keeping up or professional networking and skill set if we ever need to transition to a different job (or get laid off and then need to be able to quickly find a new job.))
RE: Support job. How not to get stuck and move to dev work?
- Make goals visible. Have a visual reminder in your space every day of what you actually want. What do you want in your life, for your family, in your future? Vision boards can work. Can be a printout of a word. Leon had a family member print out the amount of money they want to make in bold letters and put it on a wall to look at every day. Make the goal visible so it can't be shuffled away as you get more comfortable. Keep the goal top of mind. Adopt an identity that is different from your job. If you're in a full-time support job, and someone asks you what you do, you say you're a software engineer and you're doing a support job in between. Tie your identity to what you want to ultimately do, which will keep you focused on that goal. Identity setting very important to setting good habits and moving in the right direction. Get loved ones involved to help you stay focused on getting the goal you're working towards.

"DevOps/Security important for Full-Stack jobs, or is 100Devs enough?"
- DevOps and security could be worthwhile to explore if you're interested, but probably won't be the difference-maker in getting you a job
-- Try not to get distracted by this stuff if you really want a job, especially in software engineering. DevOps or security would get you a software engineering job, a better SWE job, or an SWE job faster. It is typically just a distraction
-- Focus on the proven stuff from 100Devs that works if you want to get the SWE job ASAP.
"How should I go about writing log of wins at the job?"
- Bell chart meme. Apple notes to more complexity with Obsidian, etc., and then ultimately realize the simple thing you started with was the thing you're most likely to use. Just keep it simple. Apple Note or equivalently simple that you can put in the doc, either weekly or immediately. Best way to remember and keep track so that you can use them in your performance reviews, and not try to remember every 6 months or a year when you've forgotten most of the wins, or at the least have forgotten most of the details of those wins. Over time, if you do it every week, that's 52 chances as opposed to the one that people often do at the end of the year. Leon recommends putting it on your calendar weekly. Lots more shots on goal, and you're much more likely to get the goals that can really make a difference when you can bring them up at your performance review.

"What are your thoughts on using a framer or webflow template for a portfolio? Going heavy into the hunt and would like a nice out of the box look"
Leon prefers a template we can do on our own. WebFlow is also expensive. Save money, and have something we can show off better. Astro or Tailwind template. Same amount of speed, but it ends up being code that we've written and tends to be slightly better. Long-term better to have these self-built options vs. something fast like framer or webflow. Very easy with Tailwind now to get something that looks nice out of the box, and we can talk about Tailwind during interviews.
Supporting role vs. SWE role? Supporting is 3-4x income.
- Leon: Take it, but try not to let it be a big opportunity cost. Don't get too comfortable if being an SWE is your ultimate goal. If something is on the table for 3-4x your income, take it regardless, and then use that added financial stability to pursue SWE more seriously
"I think i went through a final round a couple hours ago.... Do I follow up with the recruiter? or one of the 3 people I did the interview with? "
- VERY IMPORTANT: Everyone gets a follow-up and a thank you. **Every person you talk to**: "Had a wonderful time, [thank them for their time and for the connection.]"
-- Costs nothing to be polite, and it really does make a difference. Interviews are another networking opportunity
- You can bomb an interview and then get people to like you, and you're much more likely to get an interview offer months later and ultimately get the job. Always follow up. Thank you messages get jobs

Alec: New job about three weeks ago. Not a development job. Cable technician job. Helping install cable at houses
-- 4x his income, but he wants to get out of it as quickly as possible to get back to SWE. The environment he is in is not software-centric. Much more focused on the cable technician/field role, even if it encourages growth and networking in that field. Alec doesn't want to risk seeming too fancy for his job
- Leon: Growth should not come from your job. If you want to grow in SWE, it's probably going to happen outside of 9-5. You hit the jackpot if you can get this on job, but for most, it's happening outside of your workday. Internally, get to know all other teams that are doing SWE-related stuff. Can have virtual meetings with people in the company that you reach out to internally via company communication (eg, Slack). Always easier to transfer with the blessing of our manager. 3+ months on job, reach out to your manager about how you're crushing goals. Ask if your SWE skill set can help out the team in any way. Get them sold that your SWE skill set can help the manager look good, and then use that proven performance to help them help you make the transition you want to make. Reaching out within the company to SWE contacts doesn't need manager's blessing, and it's typically a different thing. Can let manager know you're interested in SWE work and would love to connect with more at the company who work in the field, so that they're not surprised, but Leon finds this to be two separate things, just like if you were running into people at an in-person company. Leon thinks that now that people are remote, it's pretty easy to connect with people virtually within the company, so Alec should be sure to reach out and connect with other people in the company in the line of work that he's hoping to transition into. "I'm Alec, and we're all remote, so I'm trying to connect with more people at our company. I would love to talk to you about the work you're doing. Is there a good time we can do that?" Many people may not say yes, but we won't find the ones who will if we're not out there asking.

Daniel Hopkins: New to 100Devs. Applying and got a job, but how does he go through 100Devs now that he has a full-time job? Unemployed for an entire year. Fearful he will be let go, even if he thinks that might be irrational. Nothing wrong with the job so far, but he wants to keep his options open.
Leon: The Biggest mistake is getting a job and then stopping everything in terms of growth and development. Keep doing the stuff you were doing, but probably in smaller amounts. One meetup a week, 2-3 coffee chats per week. That way, when it's time for us to need a network, it's there, and has compounded over time. Smaller pieces will add up, and we'll have that safety net if we need it. Every 6 months, people should be ready to interview. An important part of being an SWE is always being ready and not getting too comfortable (and deconditioning our skills). Every 6 months, we should interview, regardless. Helps us see what opportunities are out there and see where our weak points are so that we know what we need to develop. Too many people wait 2-3 years to get that feedback, and then they have so much to work on that it can be overwhelming, especially if we've just been laid off or something and are desperate to find a new role. Small bits every week can keep you ready, so you have less work to do when you need to do it.
