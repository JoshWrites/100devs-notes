What I recall, in reverse chronological: don't sweat the interview process too much, even if getting to the interview stage and hopefully doing well based on good preparation is definitely something worth celebrating. You might think you do really well, or really poorly, and it may work out vastly differently than what you expected. No worries! Determine what you want to take forward and apply to future interviews, and what was weird or went poorly that you're going to leave behind with that potentially unsuccessful experience. (Maybe you opted not to advocate for yourself in the face of negative feedback because you felt shy, and next time you can leave that shyness behind and make sure not only are you getting meaningful feedback but also that they actually are telling you about +your+ interview. Leon said in +many+ cases RC (Resilient Coders) students will get very odd interview feedback and it will turn out there was a mix up and the RC student is being evaluated based on the interview of an entirely different candidate in the pool. Don't just walk away without asking for clarification or further feedback potentially solely for this reason. Maybe there has been a mix up!)

  

Regardless, Leon finds that the getting too caught up with the interview outcomes during the job hunt is one of the biggest factors that cause people to burn out and quit. (Getting frustrated learning CSS or JavaScript, in that order, is probably the other big hurdle.) Don't ride that rollercoaster. It's just going to make you nauseous. Focus on what's in your control, not on what's not. We have no control over how interviews happen or how they turn out. We have control over going through the 100Devs process of getting interviews and preparing for interviews, and as long as we're being analytical to make sure we're doing the right things well, the paid opportunities will materialize eventually, but we cannot control when they do. We can control our behaviors, though.

New topic: Social Media:

It's all very likely fake or carefully controlled posturing, so please keep that in mind if you start getting sucked into it. Leon timeboxes his social media time and his news time, and doesn't put in at the beginning of the day so that he has time to get ready to experience it without starting his day with negativity. The algorithm is designed to keep you engaged so it can either get more data from you to sell to other people, or directly sell you stuff. Please keep that in mind as you're getting very emotionally charged content.

  

Also, remember that internet strangers likely stop thinking about you almost immediately, so if some stranger online gives you negative feedback, just let it wash off you like water. (Leon used the analogy "Dust in the Wind".) We don't know the full names of our eight great grandparents in most cases, let alone very many details about them, so some internet stranger, or even someone much less connected to us, is likely not thinking about us at all shortly after we interacted with them, so don't stress out about what they might be thinking of you or give them any psychological real estate that you could otherwise use more productively, either.

Some might just be sharing, especially with people they care about, and other people are selling themselves or trying to impress people with the best possible version of their lives they can muster, which may or may not be true.

Other things to cover:

  

Beginner's mind/attitude (GREAT for coffee chats and meetups, since people love talking to those who are very interested in what they want to talk about and are often at meetups because they want to help the next generation (or inspire that generation to continue to be interested in, support, and maintain the things and technologies they like), but much less great for the job hunt, where you want to portray yourself as someone who is extremely competent, can succeed right from the start, and is a good risk (in terms of investments by clients or employers))

Also, NETWORKING IS HUGE! Huddle started with people, including Leon, talking about how networking often gets you freelancing, and freelancing is one of the best ways to consistently earn money when you need it, especially to put food on the table, because you have much more control over how much or how little freelancing you do, and when you do it, vs. a more stable, long-term job (Michael also remembers a freeCodeCamp podcast where the guest talk about how she +prefers+ freelancing solely for this increased flexibility and control over her schedule and level of commitment.)
